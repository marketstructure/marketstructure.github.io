  • Starbucks announced a major restructuring of its China operations on November 4, forming a joint venture with Beijing-based Boyu Capital in which Boyu will acquire up to 60% of Starbucks' retail operations in China for approximately $4 billion. The partnership, which values Starbucks' total China business at over $13 billion, aims to accelerate expansion from 8,000 to potentially 20,000 stores over time, with Boyu providing deep local market knowledge particularly for expansion into smaller cities. Starbucks will retain a 40% stake and continue to own and license the brand while benefiting from ongoing licensing economics over the next decade.​
  • Chinese consumers are increasingly embracing Japanese budget restaurant chains as economic pressures intensify, with brands like Saizeriya, Sushiro, and Torikizoku experiencing strong growth by offering what analysts describe as "affordable dignity". These chains, which survived Japan's decades-long deflation, are exporting their recession-tested formula of low prices and standardized operations to considerable success in China's deflationary environment. Saizeriya plans to double its China outlets from 500 to 1,000 by 2035, while Sushiro's parent company aims to expand overseas locations from 200 to 320 by next year, with most new outlets in China. Menu items at these establishments cost significantly less than comparable local options—Saizeriya offers black pepper beef tomato pasta for 14 yuan ($1.97) versus hot pot restaurants where per capita spending can exceed 100 yuan.​
  • Signs of stabilization emerged in China's luxury and premium beauty segments after an extended downturn, though executives remain cautious about declaring a full recovery. LVMH reported mainland China turned positive in Q3 2025 after two quarters of decline, helping drive the luxury giant's first quarterly sales growth this year. Estée Lauder's mainland China sales surged 9% in its latest quarter—the fastest among all regions—while L'Oréal posted mid-single-digit growth, marking the first positive North Asia growth for L'Oréal in two years. However, L'Oréal CEO Nicolas Hieronimus cautioned that "one quarter doesn't make a trend," and industry analysts note the recovery is being partly driven by platform-sponsored discounts that may not be sustainable long-term.​
  • China unveiled significant enhancements to Hainan's offshore duty-free shopping policy effective November 1, expanding the product catalog from 45 to 47 categories to include pet supplies, portable musical instruments, mini drones, and small appliances. More notably, the policy now permits six categories of Chinese-made products—including silk scarves, clothing, footwear, coffee, ceramics, and tea—to enter duty-free shops while enjoying value-added and consumption tax exemptions. First-day sales under the new policy reached nearly 80 million yuan ($11 million), marking a 6.1% increase from the previous day, with domestic brands increasingly taking center stage as some items are approximately 15% cheaper thanks to tax exemptions.​
  • CP AXTRA, operator of Makro and Lotus's chains, announced a strategic technology partnership with Cainiao (Alibaba's logistics arm) on November 4 to drive retail-tech expansion across Thailand and Southeast Asia. The collaboration has already delivered measurable results: following the rollout of the jointly-developed iWMS pick-and-pack system across Makro stores, efficiency improved by 140%, while daily delivery capacity expanded from 15,000 to 100,000 orders when combined with CP AXTRA's 4,000-vehicle fleet and Transportation Management System. The partnership has helped drive CP AXTRA's e-commerce business share from 3% two years ago to 30% currently, and the companies are advancing an AI-based planogram solution to optimize in-store merchandising.​
  • At least two bidders entered the race to acquire South Korean hypermarket chain Homeplus by the October 31 preliminary deadline, including AI firm Harex InfoTech, which is reportedly preparing to raise approximately $2 billion from the United States to finance its acquisition bid. Homeplus, which was bought by MBK Partners from Tesco in 2015, filed for court receivership in March after years of declining sales and liquidity pressures, with the winning bidder required to assume debts including $940 million owed to Meritz Financial Group and $405.8 million in preferred shares held by the National Pension Service. Due diligence for qualified bidders is scheduled from November 3-21, with final bids due November 26.​
