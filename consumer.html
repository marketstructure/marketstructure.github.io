  • Recent attention has been dominated by the US-China trade deal announced on Monday, which has significantly reshaped short-term consumer business strategies across Asia. The agreement temporarily reduces tariffs on Chinese imports to the US from 145% to 30% for 90 days, while China cuts duties on US goods from 125% to 10%. This partial détente has provided immediate relief to Chinese exporters facing existential threats, with some manufacturers shelving planned job cuts and production closures. However, the reprieve is notably limited – the new 30% rate still represents the steepest tariff barrier since the 1930s, and significant uncertainty remains about what follows after the 90-day cooling period ends in August.
  • Retailers are now racing to capitalize on this window by expediting shipments during the suspension period, though container rates are expected to surge due to high demand, potentially pushing actual costs up by as much as 40% despite lower tariff rates.
  • Against this backdrop, Burberry announced plans to cut 1,700 jobs globally – approximately 18% of its workforce – after reporting an annual net loss of £75 million ($100 million) compared to last year's £270 million profit. The luxury retailer warned that the economic environment has become "more uncertain in light of geopolitical developments," specifically referencing how US tariffs risk dampening consumer confidence. Despite these challenges, Burberry's adjusted operating profit of £26 million exceeded analysts' expectations of £11 million, sending shares up over 8% in London trading.
  • In significant expansion moves, China's largest coffee chain Luckin Coffee is establishing its first US presence in New York's East Village. This marks a remarkable comeback for the company that faced fraud charges and Chapter 15 bankruptcy in 2020, and represents a growing trend of Asian companies opening their first US locations despite trade tensions. South Korean retailers are also looking overseas as domestic growth stalls.
