  • China's Fund Industry Reaches Record Scale as Consolidation Accelerates: AMAC data reported by Caixin this week showed China's mutual fund industry surpassed 38.6 trillion RMB in AUM, a fresh all-time high, with the total number of registered funds reaching 13,821 — a net increase of 96 in the latest month alone. Private fund assets also hit records, reflecting a broader industry expansion that has been building since 2025, when public funds grew by over 5 trillion RMB — their strongest annual gain in at least three years. Against this backdrop of scale, regulatory-driven consolidation is now visibly underway: Guotai Haitong Securities-controlled Huaan Fund Management (~800 billion RMB AUM, China's 16th-largest mutual fund manager) and its smaller affiliate HFT Investment Management (~250 billion RMB AUM, partly owned by BNP Paribas) are reported to be merging, with a final plan expected by end-March or early April. The driver is a CSRC rule barring a single entity from holding controlling stakes in two mutual fund managers simultaneously — a constraint that Guotai Haitong inherited following its absorption of Haitong Securities. The merger would create a combined entity of over 1 trillion RMB in AUM and is part of a broader regulatory push to consolidate China's fragmented fund management landscape into fewer, more globally competitive institutions.

  • CIC's Attempted Wall Street Re-entry Hits Geopolitical Headwinds: Bloomberg reported this week that China Investment Corporation had held discussions in recent weeks with Blackstone and TPG about resuming US private equity allocations — a significant reversal from the posture CIC adopted after Trump's return to power, when it sold off approximately $1 billion in stakes across eight US managers including Carlyle and Blackstone through end-2025. The renewed engagement was driven by anticipation of a Trump visit to China and signs of an economic thaw between the two governments. However, according to Bloomberg, CIC subsequently suspended several of those discussions after the US military action against Iran unsettled the diplomatic environment and led to a postponement of the US-China summit. CIC's reallocation posture seemingly remains live, but is highly conditional on a geopolitical backdrop that has proven volatile week to week.

  • Hong Kong Moves to Reform Carried Interest Tax Regime: The Financial Times reported this week that Hong Kong's Financial Services and Treasury Bureau is preparing legislation — expected before the Legislative Council "imminently" — that would significantly expand the scope of investments qualifying for carried interest tax treatment. Currently limited to private equity, the proposed bill would extend relief to private credit, digital assets, overseas real estate, and interests in non-corporate private entities. A government spokesperson confirmed the amendment is targeted for introduction by mid-2026, while stressing that general service bonuses remain taxable at the standard profits tax rate. This is the most substantive structural policy development for Hong Kong's asset management industry in several years.

  • Blackstone Asia Capital Deployment Headlines: Blackstone Capital Partners Asia III — which began marketing in 2024, hit its $10 billion target by 2025, and retained roughly 90% of prior investors with average re-up allocations up ~30% — is now above $12 billion and approaching final close near its $12.9 billion hard cap. Separately, Nikkei Asia reported Blackstone intends to allocate $15 billion to Japanese real estate over three years, targeting data centers, logistics, and hotels as the weak yen sustains deal attractiveness and Chinese market alternatives grow less appealing. The two announcements together represent the single largest concentration of Asia-focused private capital deployment reported this week.
