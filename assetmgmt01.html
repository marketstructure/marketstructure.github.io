  • Gulf sovereign capital embeds further into China. Saudi Arabia’s Public Investment Fund has opened a Shanghai office, its second in mainland China after Beijing, to enhance its ability to originate and execute outbound deals in the Chinese market. The new office, alongside its existing presence in Hong Kong and other global financial centres, signals that one of the world’s largest sovereign wealth funds intends to treat China as a core deployment geography rather than an opportunistic satellite, reinforcing the importance of onshore access points for capturing Gulf–China capital flows.
  • Regional banks and global houses sharpen Asia wealth franchises. OCBC’s Indonesian subsidiary has agreed to acquire HSBC Indonesia’s wealth and retail business, adding roughly 336,000 customers and S$6.6 billion of AUM, and building on its earlier acquisition of Bank Commonwealth Indonesia to accelerate its push into a fast-growing ASEAN wealth market under a Singapore-headquartered balance sheet. Separately, HSBC’s international wealth and premier banking unit reported around US$39 billion of net new money and total wealth balances of about US$1.57 trillion as of March, with Asia as the primary earnings engine, underscoring that global incumbents are likewise committing fresh balance sheet and product capacity to the region’s cross-border wealth opportunity rather than retrenching. 

  • Asian pensions widen private-markets implementation. Taiwan’s Bureau of Labour Funds reported first-quarter investment income of about NT$464.5 billion and a 5.67% return across its pension and annuity portfolios, reflecting the stabilising effect of diversified exposure across domestic and overseas equities, bonds and alternatives. In Malaysia, LTAT is preparing its first private-credit allocation and is now selecting external managers for a strategy targeting high single-digit returns with stronger downside protection. In South Korea, Teachers’ Pension has launched a 100 billion won domestic VC blind-fund tender, with commitments of up to 20 billion won per manager and up to five firms to be selected, showing that public pension capital is being deployed through more specialised private-markets sleeves rather than a single broad alternatives bucket.

  • Evergreen distribution formats. Evergreen private equity structures are moving into Asia’s mainstream product architecture. J.P. Morgan Asset Management has launched a private equity evergreen strategy for Asia Pacific investors, while Temasek-backed Azalea is preparing its own evergreen private equity fund to broaden access through a more flexible, open-ended format, indicating that regional private-markets manufacturers are adapting vehicles for wealth distribution and semi-liquid demand rather than relying solely on traditional drawdown funds.

  • Taiwan advances a controlled opening to crypto ETFs. Taiwan’s Financial Supervisory Commission is examining whether to allow retail investors to buy overseas cryptocurrency ETFs, extending a regime under which institutions and professional investors with at least NT$30 million in net assets have already been able to access foreign virtual-asset ETFs, including bitcoin funds, through 13 local securities firms since late 2024. Cumulative turnover in overseas bitcoin ETFs has reached nearly NT$10 billion, and both the regulator and Yuanta Securities Investment Trust emphasise that KYC/KYP processes and existing investor familiarity with volatility should limit capital flight risks even if retail access is approved. In parallel, the FSC is building a framework under the forthcoming Virtual Asset Service Act to permit domestic bitcoin ETFs, with rules expected as early as the second half of 2026, signalling that Taiwan is moving towards a regulated, exchange-traded wrapper for crypto exposure rather than pushing activity offshore.

  • Hong Kong alternatives and income partnerships. Janus Henderson Group and Hong Kong-based Sun Hung Kai & Co have formed a strategic partnership to co-develop and distribute alternative investment strategies, including strategic capital seeding and direct opportunities across public and private markets with a specific focus on Asia Pacific clients and deal flow. Aberdeen Investments has separately partnered with CCB (Asia) to distribute a short-dated income fund with monthly payouts and HKD, CNH and USD share classes, using a Chinese bank’s Hong Kong platform to push cash-plus fixed income solutions through a large retail and mass-affluent network. Together these moves underline Hong Kong’s continued role as a manufacturing and distribution hub where global managers rely on local balance sheets and channels to scale alternatives and yield products into regional wealth pools.
