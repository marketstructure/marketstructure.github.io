Asian markets are entering a new phase of structural sophistication. Expanded derivatives toolkits, accelerating ETF issuance, and deepening cross‑border connectivity are giving participants more nuanced ways to navigate volatility. The early‑March volatility event — including circuit breakers across multiple markets and the KOSPI’s largest single‑day decline on March 4 — provided the sharpest test of these developments so far this year.


This turbulence emerged after an exceptionally liquid start to 2026. China’s SSE and SZSE averaged US$412.7 billion in daily turnover in January, frequently setting new records driven by domestic activity, with volumes remaining elevated into March. Korea’s KRX reached an all‑time high of US$22.2 billion ADT in February, up 19% month‑on‑month on semiconductor strength. HKEX posted US$31.6 billion in ADT, contributing to a 17% year‑on‑year rise in the first two months of the year. India’s NSE cash segment hit a 16‑month high near US$14 billion in January. Japan recorded its highest‑ever February derivatives trading value alongside record ETF and single‑stock options activity, while Taiwan sustained turnover near US$28–29 billion.

The aim is to give financial executives a broad, integrated view of key developments across the region, offering context that reaches beyond any single asset class or area of focus.

Equity Primary Markets

A Resurgent Start to 2026

Asia’s IPO market has carried strong 2025 momentum into early 2026, with larger, higher‑profile offerings across technology and energy transition even as execution remains selective in some markets. Hong Kong, mainland China and India are all contributing meaningful deal flow, but with different mixes of sector focus, valuation discipline and aftermarket performance.
  • On HKEX, primary activity has accelerated sharply. In the first two months of 2026, 24 companies listed on the Main Board and GEM versus 10 a year earlier, while IPO fundraising jumped from HK$8.0 billion to HK$89.2 billion. By early March total IPO fundraising had surpassed HK$97 billion—around ten times the comparable 2025 period.

  • Mainland exchanges remain key venues for industrial and energy‑transition capital‑raising. Zhejiang Zhenshi New Material, a leading supplier of fibre‑reinforced composites for wind and photovoltaic applications, raised roughly CNY 2.92 billion in its January Shanghai IPO to fund capacity expansion and R&D. These A‑share deals complement the offshore activity by providing scale funding for China’s clean‑energy and advanced‑manufacturing ecosystem.

  • India’s primary market has started 2026 more cautiously after a record 2025, but continues to print sizeable transactions. Brookfield‑backed Clean Max Enviro Energy Solutions, the country’s largest commercial and industrial renewables platform, raised about US$341 million (INR 31 billion) in its March Mumbai listing—India’s largest IPO year‑to‑date—though the stock fell around 16–18% on debut amid weak retail participation and softer broader sentiment. Forecasts from investment banks and EY still point to India generating potentially US$20–25 billion in IPO proceeds over 2026.
Technology, AI and consumption remain the dominant themes in early 2026 issuance. In Hong Kong, Chinese AI and semiconductor names have led deal flow: MiniMax Group raised about HK$4.82 billion (roughly US$620 million) and doubled on debut; Zhipu AI raised HK$4.35 billion (~US$560 million); and GPU designer Iluvatar CoreX secured around HK$3.5–3.7 billion (~US$475 million) in a heavily oversubscribed IPO. Beyond tech, Muyuan Foods’ H‑share offering raised approximately HK$10.68 billion (around US$1.4 billion), currently the largest completed IPO in the city this year. Taken together, early‑2026 deal flow suggests a market that has shifted toward fewer but larger, thematically coherent offerings.

Around Asia
  • Cross-Border Listing Quality and GBA Connectivity: The CSRC is raising standards for cross-border listings, shifting focus from issuance volume toward investability. Chairman Wu Qing used the Two Sessions on March 6 to announce elevated listing standards for the ChiNext board and direct enforcement against follow-on financing mechanisms historically used for cash extraction rather than genuine capital expenditure. The CSRC is also advancing a proposal to mandate a minimum market capitalization threshold for mainland entities pursuing dual listings in Hong Kong. HKEX itself reported a 36% jump in 2025 net profit to HK$17.75 billion, driven by surging trading volumes and a recovery in IPO activity. Separately, HSBC Asia Chairman Peter Wong proposed an "IPO Connect" programme on March 3 that would permit cross-border retail subscriptions across the Greater Bay Area.

  • Hong Kong Regulators Raid Major Brokerages Amid IPO Boom Scrutiny: Hong Kong authorities raided the equity capital markets divisions of Citic Securities' local unit and Guotai Junan International on March 10, detaining at least one senior executive for questioning and seizing documents, in what marks a high-profile escalation of scrutiny over the city's booming IPO market. The raids follow a January 30 SFC circular that identified "serious deficiencies" in sponsor work—including poor due diligence, over-stretched principals, and unqualified staff—suspending vetting on 16 applications and ordering comprehensive reviews by 13 named sponsors amid warnings that "gatekeeping may have been eroded in the eager pursuit of deal volume." The SFC has since tightened RA6 licensing standards, conducting more rigorous on-site inspections of IPO principals, with new licensees falling sharply to just 28 in February from a monthly average of 235 in the latter half of 2025.
     
  • KRX KOSDAQ Reforms: Quality Thresholds, Delistings, and IPO Pressures: Korea Exchange is tightening entry and exit standards on KOSDAQ simultaneously. On March 9, KRX reduced the number of specialized technology evaluation agencies from 26 to 16, concentrating vetting among more established firms. On the exit side, KRX announced on February 19 a dedicated Delisting Intensive Management Task Force running through June 2027, shortening the maximum improvement period from 18 to 12 months, moving capital impairment reviews to a semiannual basis, and reducing the cumulative disclosure penalty threshold from 15 to 10 points — changes that could expose 150–220 firms to removal. The FSC has framed these exit reforms as integral to restoring investor confidence in the new issue market, where more than 10 companies are currently in subscription or pre-listing stages across KOSDAQ and KOSPI. Separately, voluntary put-back options granted to retail investors during the 2025 IPO boom are now being exercised following early-March market swings, straining underwriter balance sheets. Progress on dual-listing guidelines — designed to restrict uncompensated spin-off structures — faces further delay pending a Democratic Party of Korea hearing on March 25.

  • Indonesia IDX: IPO Standards Reform and MSCI Re-inclusion Pathway: The sweeping reform package announced by IDX and OJK in response to the late-January MSCI warning has moved into implementation. The focus now is on execution risk and the May 2026 MSCI decision. Acting IDX Chief Jeffrey Hendrik has set up readiness-based compliance batching — one to three years depending on each issuer's capacity to release shares — with OJK projecting 70–75% of the 267 non-compliant companies meeting the new 15% minimum free-float within the first year. The revised Regulation I-A tiered listing rules (25% / 20% / 15% free-float by market-cap band, with hard floors of 300 million shares and 10,000 SID holders for Main Board) apply immediately to new listings. For the primary market, the reforms raise the practical bar for IPO candidates: sub-scale issuers that cannot meet distribution floors may be redirected to the Development Board or deterred altogether. The outstanding question is whether the pace of compliance — and MSCI's own assessment at its May review — is sufficient to lift the index's reclassification pause and restore Indonesia's weighting trajectory in EM benchmarks, which would be the decisive catalyst for the passive inflows the reforms are designed to attract.
  • SGX: Dual-Track Pipeline Strategy Targets China Tech and ASEAN Issuers — SGX is actively courting China and ASEAN technology firms to list, with the mid-2026 launch of the SGX-Nasdaq Global Listing Board — a joint initiative enabling companies with S$2 billion+ market cap to dual-list using a single prospectus — positioned as the primary catalyst for higher-growth names that would not otherwise consider Singapore as a standalone venue. The framework directly addresses a structural deficit in which delistings have consistently outpaced new listings for over a decade. It is complemented by MAS's S$5 billion Equity Market Development Programme, which invests in SGX-listed equities via external fund managers — S$2.85 billion of which has already been allocated — to build baseline liquidity and attract institutional capital. Despite reporting strong profits in its recent results, SGX faces continued pressure to convert pipeline momentum into completed transactions.
ETF Landscape

Further Acceleration in ETF Issuance

The Asian ETF market continues its strong growth trajectory into 2026, with 131 new ETF launches recorded through early March—an 18% increase compared to the same period in 2025. Mainland China remains the epicenter of activity, with the Shanghai Stock Exchange (SHSE) and Shenzhen Stock Exchange (SZSE) together accounting for approximately 50% of all regional issuance. This SSE/SZSE dominance reflects China's strategic push to develop domestic passive investment products, particularly in technology and semiconductor sectors aligned with national policy priorities. The 2025 calendar year saw an extraordinary 800 ETF launches across Asia—an 81% surge from 443 in 2024—indicating sustained structural demand for exchange-traded products that shows no signs of abating.

Geographic & Exchange Concentration

The geographic concentration of ETF issuance reveals distinct market characteristcs. Beyond Mainland China's ~50% share, South Korea and Japan represent the next tier, followed closely by India. Australia, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore collectively contribute the remaining ~20%, with Singapore notably registering only 2 launches YTD. AI & Chip Design ETFs are highly represented on Chinese exchanges, reflecting state-aligned technology investment priorities, while Fixed Income products are highly represented on the Tokyo Stock Exchange, reflecting Japan's mature bond market infrastructure. Hong Kong Connect-themed ETFs (15 launches) demonstrate strong investor appetite for cross-border mainland equity access, with launches split between SHSE and SZSE.

Thematic & Liquidity Insights

The thematic landscape reveals a striking dominance of AI and technology-focused strategies. Over 40% of all 2026 YTD launches are tied to AI, semiconductor, technology, or digital themes. Metals & Mining ETFs command the highest average market capitalization at over $100M, suggesting institutional investor preference for commodity exposure amid global supply chain concerns. Conversely, Financial Sector ETFs demonstrate the highest average daily trading value at $35.5M—driven primarily by Taiwan bank ETFs with high liquidity. The Index/Broad Market theme leads in launch volume (17 ETFs), reflecting continued demand for passive core exposure across the region. These liquidity disparities carry important implications: while China-domiciled ETFs benefit from deep domestic trading pools, smaller regional exchanges face structural challenges in building sustainable secondary market liquidity for newer products.

Issuer Landscape

The issuer concentration is notable—E Fund Management leads decisively with 9 new launches YTD, followed by Asset Management One and a cluster of Chinese managers including Penghua, Fullgoal, and ICBC UBS. Chinese asset managers collectively command over 70% of new product launches, underscoring the domestic industry's maturation, scale advantages, and alignment with regulatory encouragement of passive investment vehicles. Japanese issuers (Daiwa, Global X Japan, Nomura) show strength in specialized fixed income and thematic products, while Indian and Korean managers demonstrate growing regional ambitions with diversified product suites.

On March 9 southbound funds poured a record HK$37 billion+ into Hong Kong-listed ETFs, with the largest Hang Seng Tech Index ETF (513180) alone attracting over 9.3 billion yuan in net inflows year-to-date and posting sharp outperformance. The flows reflect renewed foreign optimism toward Chinese technology names and have tightened spreads while boosting secondary-market liquidity in the connected universe. Passive EM and China-focused trackers experienced automatic reallocation into the favoured constituents, creating arbitrage opportunities for market makers and incremental index turnover for issuers in the Hang Seng Tech basket.

  • HKEX Commodity ETF Expansion: E Fund Global Gold Miners Listing — E Fund Management (Hong Kong) listed its global gold miners ETF on HKEX on February 11, 2026, providing investors with targeted exposure to an international basket of gold-mining equities. The launch complements rising commodity appetite in the Greater China investor base and adds another liquid instrument to HKEX’s growing ETF roster. Market makers benefit from new hedging and arbitrage flows linked to the underlying mining stocks, while passive and thematic funds gain an additional tool for precious-metals allocation without direct physical or futures exposure.

  • Korea: Active KOSDAQ ETFs, Retail Leverage and Platform Competition: Korea Exchange listed the country’s first two active KOSDAQ ETFs on March 10 — Timefolio Asset Management’s TIME KOSDAQ Active and Samsung Asset Management’s KoAct KOSDAQ Active — marking a structural shift toward active strategies within an ecosystem that has until now been dominated by passive products. Foreign and institutional flows rotated into these growth-focused vehicles targeting semiconductor and AI alpha, while heavy retail concentration in passive KOSPI-linked ETFs has introduced a localised volatility feedback loop: large inflows trigger rapid market-maker hedging that amplifies intraday swings in underlying large-caps. Retail demand for leveraged products has surged — constrained by a domestic 2x cap, Korean retail accounts have increasingly sought exposure through US-listed triple-leveraged vehicles, contributing to record daily ETF turnover. NEXTRADE, the alternative trading platform that marked its first anniversary on March 3, has outlined plans to introduce ETF trading and fractional execution by Q4 2026, adding competitive pressure on KRX through extended hours and fee compression.

  • Korea: FSS Intervention: The Financial Supervisory Service has ramped up oversight amid the volatility. On March 5 it warned against misleading ETF advertising that understates fees, obscures total cost metrics beyond headline TER, or implies guaranteed returns. On March 12 the FSS convened commodity-market experts and directed firms to strengthen risk disclosures for oil and commodity-linked ETFs and ETNs amid the surge in oil prices and 11-fold increase in related ETP trading volumes (to daily averages of 167.6 billion won). Regulators highlighted potential divergences between market prices and intrinsic values during heightened volatility, particularly for leveraged and inverse products.
  • Singapore SGX ETF Momentum: Record Turnover and Debut of First Home-Grown Physical Gold ETF — SGX posted record ETF trading volumes in January 2026, with monthly turnover doubling to S$1.4 billion and total ETF AUM reaching S$19.1 billion, capping the strongest start to any year on record. Building on this momentum, LionGlobal Investments will list Singapore’s first home-grown physical gold ETF on March 26, offering investors direct exposure to London Bullion Market gold without currency or custody frictions. The combined developments enhance product depth and institutional participation in the S$19 billion ETF segment, delivering tighter spreads and improved price discovery for market makers while expanding allocation options for passive and active funds benchmarked to SGX-linked benchmarks.

  • India: SEBI Proposes ETF Pricing Overhaul Following Gold/Silver Anomaly. SEBI issued a consultation paper on February 12 proposing a structural overhaul of ETF base pricing and price band frameworks, triggered by a documented 21.26% single-day pricing dislocation in gold and silver ETFs during late-January volatility — a direct consequence of the existing T-2 NAV reference system, which creates a one-day lag that left ETF prices misaligned with rapidly moving underlying assets. The proposal would shift base price determination to T-1 metrics and replace the uniform ±20% fixed band with differentiated dynamic bands: an initial ±10% for equity and debt ETFs (expandable to ±20% with cooling-off periods) and ±6% for gold and silver ETFs (with staged relaxation aligned to commodity derivatives limits). A pre-open session for commodity ETFs is also under consideration to facilitate equilibrium price discovery. Separately, SEBI mandated from April 1, 2026 that gold and silver ETFs shift valuation from LBMA benchmarks to domestic exchange-published spot prices, further aligning ETF pricing with real-time Indian market conditions. The public comment period closed March 6, with implementation timelines pending.

  • BoJ Divestment Pressure & FSA Digital Asset Integration Roadmap: Japanese lawmakers are escalating pressure on the Bank of Japan to formalize a definitive liquidation glide path for its $615 billion legacy ETF portfolio, aiming to unwind the systemic market distortion caused by prolonged quantitative easing. While the BoJ executes opportunistic localized divestment, the unquantified scale of the overhang heavily impacts forward volatility modeling. In parallel, the Financial Services Agency (FSA) outlined a regulatory overhaul of the Investment Trust Act slated for 2028. The framework will legally classify digital assets as eligible holdings for domestic ETFs and proposes harmonizing the current punitive 55% cryptocurrency capital gains tax rate with the flat 20% levy applied to traditional equities.
Equity Market Structure

Index Rebalancings and Constituent Developments

  • MSCI China Index Rebalancing: Sectoral Tilt Toward Technology Names: MSCI’s February 2026 quarterly review, effective February 27, delivered the largest net addition of Chinese stocks to its Global Standard Indexes in nearly three years (+21 net constituents after adding 37 and removing 16), with explicit weighting increases toward technology, AI, semiconductors and autonomous-driving names including SenseTime, Pony.ai and Hesai Technology for the second consecutive quarter. The adjustments reflected a pure sectoral reallocation within the overall China country exposure rather than any aggregate weight change, as confirmed in the index-provider’s constituent list release. Passive funds benchmarked to MSCI China and ACWI indices experienced automatic rebalancing flows into the added tech constituents, with direct implications for market makers managing index-tracking arbitrage and for listed issuers in non-tech sectors facing relative underweight pressure in global EM allocations.

  • Indonesia Capital Market Reforms: Disclosure Threshold Lowered and Free-Float Mandate Accelerated Post-MSCI Warning: IDX and KSEI on March 3, 2026, began monthly publication of shareholder names for stakes above 1% across all listed issuers, down from the prior 5% threshold, directly responding to MSCI's late-January rebalancing pause over free-float opacity and suspected price coordination risks that contributed to an $80 billion JCI drawdown. OJK simultaneously enforced a phased increase in the minimum free-float requirement from 7.5% to 15%, projecting 70-75% compliance among ~956 issuers within the first year through readiness-based batching (one- to three-year windows for the ~267 non-compliant names, unlocking up to Rp187 trillion or $11 billion in potential share supply), with special notations imposed on laggards. IDX expanded liquidity-provider eligible stocks and introduced flexible incentives effective March 2, while accelerating IDX demutualization via implementing regulations targeted for completion by end-February ahead of private placement and potential IPO. S&P Dow Jones Indices proceeded with its March quarterly review despite MSCI and FTSE Russell pauses, positioning passive EM trackers for potential reweighting flows and easing compliance burdens for concentrated large-cap issuers ahead of the May MSCI decision.

  • Vietnam: Circular 08 Completes Pre-Funding Reform Ahead of FTSE EM Inclusion: Vietnam's Ministry of Finance issued Circular 08/2026/TT-BTC effective February 3, extending the non-pre-funding framework first introduced under Circular 68 in November 2024 — the structural reform that triggered FTSE Russell's reclassification of Vietnam as an Emerging Market in October 2025. Circular 08's core addition permits foreign investors to execute trades directly through international broker intermediaries, removing the requirement to engage a local Vietnamese securities firm as executing broker while retaining local custody arrangements. FTSE Russell's March 2026 interim review will assess implementation progress on direct brokerage access ahead of Vietnam's expected inclusion in the FTSE Emerging Markets Index in September 2026, an event projected to catalyse significant passive inflows.

Product and Derivatives Developments

  • KRX Signs 24-Hour Derivatives Agreements and Weighs Delay in Extended Trading Sessions: KRX signed agreements on March 9 with Eurex and ICE to enable 24-hour trading of Korea-related index derivatives and is weighing a delay of domestic pre-market (7-8 a.m.) and after-market (4-8 p.m.) sessions—originally slated for June 29—to the second half due to broker infrastructure and labor concerns. Market makers confronted intensified hedging demands and program-trading curbs, while passive index funds and retail margin accounts navigated disrupted rebalancing and amplified intraday swings. In parallel, Korea’s FSC and KRX have moved to permit single‑stock leveraged ETFs, expand weekly and other maturities for KOSPI 200 and KOSDAQ 150 index options, and introduce new option contracts on individual stocks and ETFs, broadening the local hedging toolkit and supporting more diversified covered‑call and income‑oriented ETF strategies.

  • BSE Derivatives Expansion: SEBI Approves Focused Midcap Index Futures and Options: SEBI approved BSE’s launch of index futures and options on the newly created BSE Focused Midcap Index, which tracks the top 20 mid-cap companies by free-float market capitalization, alongside contracts on the Sensex Next 30. The products expand BSE’s derivative offerings beyond Sensex and Bankex, providing market makers and proprietary desks with targeted hedging tools in the mid-cap segment and enabling more precise exposure for institutional allocators and passive strategies.

  • Indian Retail Derivatives Surveillance: SEBI Crackdown on Retail F&O Activity: SEBI accelerated its crackdown on retail-driven hyperactivity in the F&O segment. With quantitative options volume caps failing to tangibly slow participation rates by late February, SEBI Chairman Tuhin Kanta Pandey shifted focus to underlying retail catalysts. The regulator deployed its AI-driven surveillance architecture, "Sudarshan," on March 2 to systematically neutralize over 120,000 misleading posts. In parallel, structural trading costs face heightened scrutiny as institutional reports highlight the BSE’s elevated transaction fee regime.

Equity Market Development and Governance

  • Singapore Equity Market Development Programme: MAS Expands Commitment to S$6.5 Billion: MAS announced on February 12, 2026, an expansion of the Equity Market Development Programme from S$5 billion to S$6.5 billion through a S$1.5 billion top-up to the Financial Sector Development Fund, building on S$3.95 billion already deployed across nine institutional managers including JPMorgan Asset Management and Fullerton. The programme channels dedicated capital into Singapore-listed equities to lift institutional participation, trading volumes, and liquidity depth—particularly for mid- and small-caps—while catalysing third-party inflows and supporting SGX issuers amid broader structural initiatives such as SGX RegCo’s review of smaller board lots for higher-priced stocks. Early 2026 trading data are consistent with that objective: in February 2026 SGX’s securities daily average value rose 45% year‑on‑year to S$2.1 billion (a six‑year high), total securities turnover grew 30% to S$38.5 billion, small‑ and mid‑cap turnover surged 135%, and ETF turnover jumped 172% year‑on‑year to S$1.1 billion amid record net inflows and the Straits Times Index briefly crossing the 5,000 level. Passive benchmarked funds and market makers stand to benefit from improved price discovery and reduced execution slippage in the S$1 trillion equity market as the next manager cohort is appointed around mid-2026. 

  • Pan-Asian Clearing Optimization and Exchange Governance: Bourses across South and Southeast Asia executed fragmented microstructural and governance tightening. Pakistan successfully compressed its capital market settlement cycle to T+1 on February 11 to mitigate counterparty risk. Bursa Malaysia formalized a reclassification of investor segments on February 24 to clarify trading participation metrics, while the SET introduced targeted liquidity reforms to capture rebounding local volumes. In the Philippines, the SEC intervened in exchange governance, floating a strict 10-year tenure cap for broker directors to neutralize institutional entrenchment.  

  • Philippines: SEC Develops Dedicated Tech Growth Board: The Philippine Securities and Exchange Commission has initiated structural groundwork for a dedicated technology-focused growth board, targeted for launch in the first half of 2026. The initiative reflects a broader ASEAN trend — visible also in Korea's KOSDAQ quality reforms and Malaysia's Capital Market Masterplan — of exchanges creating tiered listing venues to attract high-growth technology issuers that may not meet main board capitalisation or profitability thresholds. Further details on listing eligibility criteria and governance framework are pending.
     

Volatility Safeguards

  • KRX Volatility Safeguards: Circuit Breakers Triggered Repeatedly Amid Geopolitical Shock and Turnover Surge: KRX activated level-1 circuit breakers and 20-minute trading halts on both KOSPI and KOSDAQ multiple times in early March 2026, including a record 12.06% single-day plunge on March 4 (largest percentage drop in 46 years) and an 8.11% drop on March 9 that also triggered an earlier sell-side sidecar on KOSPI 200 futures, followed by a 5% surge prompting buy-side intervention on March 10—all driven by Middle East tensions, oil price spikes above $110, and forced liquidations. These events erased over $550 billion in market capitalization within days while doubling overall stock turnover rates, with ETF daily volumes exploding nearly fivefold to an average 34 trillion won.
Fixed Income and Currencies
  • SGX: Asian Government Bond Futures Incoming: SGX announced on March 9 plans to launch government bond futures covering India, Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, and the Philippines — with contracts spanning 3-, 5-, and 10-year maturities per country, USD-settled and priced against a basket of sovereign bonds. CEO Loh Boon Chye, speaking at a conference in Florida, cited clear demand for duration risk management tools as Middle East tensions drive bond market volatility globally. The launch, expected within weeks, will meaningfully extend SGX's fixed income derivatives franchise beyond its existing JGB, SORA, and TONA futures suite.

  • Offshore RMB Utility and Regional Clearing: On February 2, 2026, Clearstream enabled offshore Chinese government bonds as eligible collateral for initial margin segregation, in direct collaboration with pioneering institutions including Crédit Agricole CIB, CITIC Securities, and Shanghai Pudong Development Bank. This upgrade integrates China’s US$26 trillion sovereign bond market more effectively into international repo and OTC derivatives margin infrastructures, enhancing capital efficiency for global participants. Regionally, SGX Group is actively exploring deeper interoperability with HKEX to expand offshore CNH (yuan) trading volume and cross-trading linkages, while advancing its new Global Listing Board in partnership with Nasdaq—scheduled for launch around mid-2026—to streamline dual listings via simplified single-prospectus processes for qualifying companies.

  • Chinese Bank De-Risking: US Treasury Concentration Guidance: Chinese regulators in early February communicated verbal guidance to some of the country's largest banks — including major state-owned and commercial institutions — advising them to limit new purchases of US Treasuries and reduce existing positions where exposure has become concentrated. The guidance, which does not apply to China's official state-held Treasury reserves and carries no specific reduction targets or deadlines, was framed by regulators as a concentration risk and market volatility management measure rather than a geopolitical signal. Chinese banks collectively hold an estimated $298 billion in USD-denominated bonds, and the directive continues a years-long structural reduction in China's Treasury exposure — which has roughly halved since 2013. For regional market structure, the guidance adds incremental pressure on cross-border fixed income flows at a moment when offshore sovereign bond markets are already navigating elevated volatility.

  • Bilateral Swap Restricting and Reserve Reliance: The U.S. Treasury and Federal Reserve formally rejected South Korea's bilateral currency swap proposal in early March, establishing a restrictive precedent for dollar liquidity access. Despite the South Korean won breaching the psychological 1,500-per-dollar threshold, Washington justified the denial by pointing to Seoul’s $1 trillion in total foreign currency assets, including $400 billion in official reserves and $500 billion held by the National Pension Service. Bank of Korea Governor Rhee Chang-yong deployed verbal interventions to temper the depreciation, highlighting that external borrowing spreads and CDS premiums remain stable near 25 basis points, indicating the sell-off stems from Middle Eastern geopolitical risk rather than fundamental insolvency. This transactional shift in U.S. monetary diplomacy forces emerging Asian markets to lean entirely on internal buffers, structurally validating the persistent physical reserve accumulation seen in jurisdictions like Malaysia, which reported a reserve climb to $127.9 billion in mid-February to guarantee 4.7 months of import cover.

  • RBI: Draft Framework to Bring NDF Activity Onshore: The Reserve Bank of India published draft directions on February 16 that would allow Authorised Dealer Category-I banks to trade foreign exchange and currency interest rate derivatives on regulated offshore electronic trading platforms — including beyond domestic market hours — as part of a structural effort to recapture rupee NDF volumes currently transacted offshore. The draft also permits AD banks to undertake rupee non-deliverable derivative contracts (NDDCs) cash-settled in rupees or foreign currency, subject to maintaining an IFSC banking unit, and allows placement of foreign currency deposits with overseas counterparties within prudential limits. Offshore platforms must be domiciled in FATF-member jurisdictions and regulated by CPMI- or IOSCO-affiliated authorities.

  • Korea: Foreign Ownership of Korean Equities Reaches 30.8% of Market Cap: Foreign ownership of Korean listed equities rose to 1,326.8 trillion won (~$918 billion) by end-2025, lifting foreign ownership's share of total market capitalisation from 27.0% to 30.8% year-on-year, driven primarily by U.S. institutional investors targeting semiconductor and AI names including Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix. The increase reflects both net foreign buying and market appreciation in Korea's technology-heavy index, sustaining elevated domestic turnover and intensifying programme trading and hedging demand for market makers managing large passive rebalancing flows.

  • Korea: Institutional Overseas Holdings Hit Record $507.83 Billion: Korean institutional investors expanded holdings of overseas securities to a record $507.83 billion at end-2025, up 20.7% or $87.24 billion year-on-year, per Bank of Korea data released March 4. Asset managers accounted for the largest share of the increase, concentrated in US technology sector equities, with foreign stocks alone rising $66 billion to $292.53 billion. Domestic retail investors have simultaneously increased offshore exposure, with early 2026 flows directed notably toward Hong Kong-listed AI names — a rotation that underscores the broadening of Korean retail participation beyond domestic markets.

  • India: SEBI Launches SWAGAT-FI Framework for Trusted Foreign Investors: SEBI introduced the Single Window Automatic and Generalised Access for Trusted Foreign Investors (SWAGAT-FI) framework on January 16, effective June 1, 2026, offering eligible low-risk institutional investors a streamlined combined FPI and FVCI registration pathway, 10-year validity periods, and reduced documentation requirements. Qualifying investors must meet objective verification criteria — focusing on regulatory standing, track record, and jurisdiction of domicile — reducing long-term entry friction for institutional capital without compromising SEBI's risk-tiering approach. The framework is SEBI's most substantive FPI onboarding reform since the 2019 FPI consolidation.

National carbon prices, which breached RMB 100 per tonne in late 2025, are stabilizing at elevated levels; rising compliance costs increase liquidity requirements for covered industrial issuers while expanding price-discovery and risk-management opportunities for market makers and carbon-focused strategies across China's expanding ETS universe.
     
  • EU CBAM Enters Definitive Phase: March 31 Authorisation Deadline and April 7 First Certificate Price: The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism entered its definitive phase on January 1, 2026, with Asian exporters of covered carbon-intensive goods now subject to binding compliance obligations.
