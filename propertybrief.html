  • Hong Kong: capitulation by existing owners is meeting opportunistic capital. June produced the first positive Grade-A office absorption since January, with 44,200 sq ft taken up – overwhelmingly by finance names – even as rents slipped another 0.5%-m/m and vacancies stayed at 13.6%. In retail, government data confirm that shop values keep sliding, with the official retail-property price index setting a fresh five-year low in July. Stressed owners are moving fast: Lai Sun is marketing its 50% stake in the Central CCB Tower, a HK$10 billion asset, to cut debt, while HSBC and Hang Seng booked a combined US$0.9 billion jump in credit-loss provisions tied to local commercial real estate. At the same time, deep discounts are drawing cash-rich buyers; Meitu co-founder Mike Cai paid just HK$750 million (-48% to compulsory-sale valuation) for a Causeway Bay redevelopment site – his second tower in three months.
  • Mainland China: Evergrande’s 18-month trading suspension expires this week, putting a formal Hong Kong delisting in motion and underscoring the sector’s inability to deliver viable restructurings. Elsewhere, property-manager Onewo accepted ¥256.7 million of Vanke-related apartments and parking spaces in lieu of overdue receivables – its second such swap inside a week – signalling tighter liquidity even at quasi-state borrowers. To keep lenders onside, Vanke itself pledged an 18.3% stake in Onewo for a ¥2.8 billion shareholder loan at just 2.34% interest, highlighting the premium that state backing now commands.
  • Singapore: active capital recycling around core income assets. Savills records S$380 million of hospitality sales in Q2 (+14% q-q), all to a BlackRock-led consortium that picked up Citadines Raffles Place and Momentus Novena, adding to its February buy of Mount Sophia. On Orchard Road, billionaire Ong Beng Seng’s HPL is quietly testing the market for majority stakes in Forum The Shopping Mall and the voco Orchard hotel at a mooted S$2 billion valuation, following URA approval for a mixed-use rebuild. Separately, Keppel’s shift from developer to asset-manager gathered pace: H1 profit rose 25% to S$431 million, underpinned by real-estate platforms and a S$14.4 billion pipeline of non-core disposals plus a S$500 million buy-back.
  • Japan: large-scale portfolio rotations pick up. Sapporo Holdings has drawn at least three bidding groups – Bain/Lone Star, Bain/Tokyu Fudosan and KKR/PAG – for its ¥400 billion (US$2.7 billion) property arm anchored by Yebisu Garden Place, with exclusivity expected by November. Capital is also redeployed within listed vehicles: Ichigo Hotel REIT is reinvesting February sale proceeds into two regional hotels for ¥5.7 billion at a 14% discount to appraisal, expanding its portfolio to 31 assets. Foreign owners are crystallising gains; CapitaLand Ascott Trust has agreed to sell Citadines Central Shinjuku for ¥25 billion (S$222.7 million), double book value, with completion targeted in Q4 and proceeds earmarked for higher-yield replacements.
  • India: Phoenix Mills doubles down on its strongest retail platform. The developer will acquire Canada Pension Plan’s 49% in Island Star Mall Developers – owner of Bangalore’s Phoenix MarketCity and 4.4 million sq ft of operating retail – for ₹5,450 crore (US$655 million) payable over three years, lifting its stake to 100%. The structure (dividends, buy-back and selective capital reduction) allows Phoenix to keep group gearing steady while locking in the entire cash flow from a portfolio that generated ₹6.2 billion EBITDA in FY 2025 and has 0.8 million sq ft of pre-funded expansion under way.
