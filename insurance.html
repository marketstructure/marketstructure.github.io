  • China: stronger sector metrics, rising earnings, and patient capital deployment. China’s insurance sector reported primary premium income of about US$345b (RMB2.3t) in Q1 2026, up 6.2% year-on-year, with claims and benefit payments rising 7.5% to US$133.4b (RMB889.3b); new policy count jumped 29% to 32.1bn, while comprehensive and core solvency ratios remained high at 181.0% and 131.9%, respectively. New China Life estimates H1 2026 unaudited net profit attributable to shareholders at CNY20.7–23.7bn (US$3.1–3.1bn), a 40–60% increase versus H1 2025. In parallel, China Life Insurance has committed RMB4.999bn into a new RMB5bn, eight‑year semiconductor-focused equity fund, established with an affiliate under Beijing’s “patient capital” push, marking its first dedicated sector fund and adding to extensive existing semiconductor exposure.  

  • Hong Kong: sales-conduct enforcement is becoming more systematic. The Insurance Authority is introducing a cross-sector reference-checking arrangement with the HKMA, allowing insurers and banks to review job applicants’ conduct history. The scheme covers more than 110,000 insurance intermediaries across around 1,000 insurance institutions and banks. The Authority also plans a more explicitly risk-based licensing approach, including licence conditions that can restrict certain business models and require more frequent reporting by intermediaries exhibiting improper or higher-risk conduct. Senior management has been made directly accountable for compliance throughout the sales process, while recent guidance also addresses online promotional activity. These measures follow the regulator’s stated intention to penalise insurers found circumventing prior curbs on broker remuneration, policy illustration rates and unsuitable sales practices.

  • Japan: capital framework overhaul, premium expansion, and evolving investment/benefit structures. Effective March 2026, Japan’s Financial Services Agency replaced the legacy Solvency Margin Ratio with the market-consistent Japan Insurance Capital Standard (J‑ICS), under which life insurers’ financial strength is assessed via the Economic Solvency Ratio; all major domestic life carriers currently report ESRs comfortably above the new 100% regulatory minimum. The shift has coincided with a sharp reported rise in life insurers’ premium income—up 104.7% to about US$239b—alongside growing use of asset‑intensive reinsurance and draft FSA rules to tighten oversight of such risk transfers and introduce simultaneous recapture stress tests. Meiji Yasuda is considering increasing its holdings of super‑long Japanese government bonds by up to ¥1tn, reflecting renewed appetite for duration under the new capital regime. 

  • South Korea: corporate restructuring and a broader health-services proposition. Hanwha Corporation secured 99.95% shareholder approval to spin off its technology and lifestyle businesses—covering units such as Hanwha Vision and Hanwha Hotel & Resorts—into Hanwha Machinery & Service Holdings, effective 1 August. The transaction separates industrial and service operations as part of the group’s governance restructuring. Separately, life insurers are extending their health propositions beyond insurance coverage: Hanwha Life has filed to operate a health-management centre in Seoul’s 63 Building, offering in-person health measurement and coaching, while Samsung Life is expanding services spanning health consultations, medical appointment booking, hospital escort, psychological counselling and caregiver support. Some services will be offered to non-policyholders. Hyundai Marine has also launched a health insurance product for customers with pre-existing medical histories, widening eligibility for a traditionally more difficult-to-underwrite segment.

  • Thailand: broker consolidation and life-sector operational upgrades. Marsh has completed the buyout of local shareholders in its 48‑year Thailand joint venture after a 48‑year partnership, taking full ownership of the entity and rebranding it as Marsh Risk Thailand, underscoring accelerating consolidation of broking platforms in ASEAN as global brokers integrate regional operations. Separately, Thailand’s life insurance association is moving to upgrade claims processes and patient-care coordination, with initiatives aimed at improving claims handling efficiency and closer alignment between insurers and healthcare providers, signalling an industry-level push to enhance service quality rather than a single‑company initiative.

  • India: foreign strategic moves and life/pension product momentum. Zurich Insurance has already agreed to acquire 51% of Kotak Mahindra General Insurance (with the option to raise its stake to 70% over time) and is now reported to be evaluating a full buyout of Kotak Mahindra Bank’s remaining interest in their general insurance joint venture, contingent on valuation negotiations. Japan’s Sompo Holdings is publicly positioning India as a strategic growth market in the wake of insurance-sector reforms that raised foreign investment limits and advanced the “Insurance for All by 2047” agenda, with plans to leverage global know‑how to expand Universal Sompo’s presence, including in broader “well‑being” segments. Generali Central Life Insurance has launched a new participating pension plan in India specifically framed as a tool to help close the country’s retirement savings gap, adding to competitive pressure in long‑term savings offerings.
