Asia Insurance Monitor – June 25, 2026

  • Mainland China: capital strengthening, cross‑border investment pilots and catastrophe protection gap. New China Life received regulatory approval to issue up to RMB 10bn (about US$1.48bn) in perpetual capital bonds in the interbank market, reinforcing its core capital base. Ping An highlighted a portfolio strategy centred on long‑duration bonds and high‑dividend equities, attributing investment performance to disciplined strategic asset allocation and active management. Asia Insurance Review reported that six life insurers are piloting the Southbound Connect investment programme, expanding onshore insurers’ access to Hong Kong and overseas assets through regulated channels.  

  • Hong Kong: strong premium growth, property line strain, cross‑border concerns and capital actions. Hong Kong’s general insurance premiums rose 8% year‑on‑year to HK$108.5bn in 2025, but domestic property damage underwriting profit fell 97.2% to HK$15.8m, with Wang Fuk Court fire claims a major driver of the deterioration in the property line. CGS International reported that Hong Kong insurers are largely shrugging off fears that Beijing’s recent crackdown on cross‑border investments will target legal mainland Chinese insurance purchases, moderating immediate concern around offshore premium flows. In the wealth‑migration segment, Aberdeen and Heng An Standard Life have launched a high‑yield bond fund designed for Hong Kong investment migrants seeking steady monthly payouts while preserving the capital required for residency. Separately, Prudential continues to execute share buybacks in Hong Kong and London and cancel repurchased shares, reducing its issued ordinary share capital under a programme of up to US$1.2bn running through 2026, with further cancellations disclosed this month. 

  • South Korea: divergent overseas strategies, pressured auto results and Lotte’s de‑risking plus AI health tie‑up. Chosunbiz highlighted mixed performance in the US market: Samsung Fire & Marine and KB Insurance have scaled back or moved to withdraw, while DB Insurance and Hyundai Marine & Fire are expanding via localisation and recent overseas deal activity, underlining divergent approaches to international growth. Separate coverage points to continued strain in Korea’s auto segment, with loss ratios rising into the mid‑80s and combined ratios above 100%, widening underwriting losses despite premium adjustments. Seoul Economic Daily reported that Lotte Insurance has halted new alternative investments for roughly five years, reduced risky assets by about KRW1.6tn and lifted its K‑ICS ratio to around 164%, as part of a capital‑strengthening and simplification drive ahead of a potential sale. In health, Lotte has also signed an agreement with Kakao Healthcare to provide AI‑enabled chronic disease management and digital health services to group health customers, combining wearables and Kakao’s “PASTA” platform with insurance propositions.

  • Southeast Asia: premium and loss dynamics, plus health and retirement protection initiatives. Malaysia’s general insurance premiums grew 6.1% in 2025 to about US$5.8bn, with motor remaining the largest line at 45.2% of the portfolio yet still generating underwriting losses, highlighting ongoing pricing and claims‑cost pressure. Malaysian Re and China Re Life have signed a reinsurance treaty to create Malaysia’s first dedicated framework for advanced cancer treatment coverage, aimed at closing a precision‑medicine protection gap and supporting richer cancer benefits in local health products. Asia Insurance Review flagged mounting credit insurance losses in Indonesia, with sector loss ratios above 100% in Q1 2026 putting pressure on general insurers, even as AM Best expects PT Asuransi Tokio Marine Indonesia to maintain strongest‑level risk‑adjusted capitalisation over the medium term. Health and ageing themes are prominent elsewhere: Thai coverage describes the Thai Life Insurance Association urging closer public‑private cooperation to address rapid medical inflation and accelerate healthcare system reforms, Ho Chi Minh City has approved a pilot allowing home‑based medical examination and treatment to be reimbursed under health insurance, focusing on elderly and mobility‑limited residents, and recent Singapore reporting highlights sizeable retirement and protection gaps as insurers broaden offerings into wealth accumulation, retirement income and legacy planning for an ageing population. 

  • India: regulatory tightening, AI risk governance and international market infrastructure. IRDAI has issued a consultation paper proposing amendments to insurance intermediary regulations: brokers, corporate agents, web aggregators and other intermediaries earning more than ₹10 crore in annual commissions would be required to disclose commission income, profits and related‑party transactions to the regulator and publish them on their websites, with per‑offence penalties raised to ₹10 crore and each policy tagged to the individual responsible for the sale to strengthen accountability and curb mis‑selling. The Reserve Bank of India released draft guidance on model risk management for banks and other regulated entities, mandating a board‑approved framework covering all models (including AI/ML), independent validation, ongoing enterprise‑level risk assessment and human oversight of AI‑driven decisions, with boards empowered to restrict, remediate or decommission models where risks are deemed excessive. On the market side, Lloyd’s has signed a new agreement with ICICI Lombard under which the Indian carrier will issue local policies for selected lines on behalf of Lloyd’s managing agents, further strengthening multinational placement capabilities in India. 

  • Japan: Insurers turn net sellers of super-long JGBs as yields hit multi-decade highs. Japanese insurers sold a net JPY201.2bn (~US$1.25bn) of government bonds with maturities exceeding 10 years in May, partially reversing the JPY327.2bn of net purchases recorded in April, as yields on super-long JGBs climbed to multi-decade highs and volatility increased. This represents a meaningful shift in ALM positioning by an investor class that has historically been among the largest structural buyers of long-duration domestic sovereign debt.
